%-------------------- ABSTRACT --------------------

\begin{abstract}
Data poisoning attacks pose a fundamental threat to machine learning systems by corrupting training data to degrade model performance. Among these, label-flip attacks—where an adversary relabels a fraction of training samples from a source class to a target class—are particularly stealthy and effective. Existing defenses often rely on heuristic filtering or post-hoc inspection, and lack a principled game-theoretic justification.

This paper presents a game-theoretic framework for studying label-flip attacks and defenses in deep neural networks. We model the attacker-defender interaction as a two-player zero-sum Stackelberg game and propose an alternating Min--Max training procedure as the defender's strategy. The attacker maximizes the model's loss by injecting poisoned labels, while the defender minimizes it through iterative retraining.

Extensive experiments on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 demonstrate that the proposed Min--Max defender consistently outperforms Spectral Signatures and SEVER across all settings. On CIFAR-10 with 50\% label-flip rate, the defender recovers 4.5 percentage points of accuracy lost to the attack. Statistical significance testing confirms that all reported improvements are reliable. The framework is general, interpretable, and provides a foundation for studying adversarial interactions in training pipelines.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
Data Poisoning, Label-Flip Attack, Game Theory, Min--Max Defense, Adversarial Machine Learning, Robust Training, Deep Neural Networks
\end{IEEEkeywords}

%-------------------- I. INTRODUCTION --------------------

\section{Introduction}

The reliability of machine learning models depends critically on the integrity of their training data. In adversarial settings, a malicious actor with access to the training pipeline can inject poisoned samples to degrade model performance, bias predictions toward specific classes, or cause targeted misclassification. Such \textit{data poisoning attacks} are a growing concern in real-world deployments, where training data is often collected from untrusted or crowd-sourced sources.

Among poisoning strategies, \textit{label-flip attacks} are particularly dangerous because they are semantically covert: the attacker relabels a fraction of samples from a source class to a target class without modifying the images themselves. The poisoned data appears visually plausible, making detection by human inspection difficult. For instance, relabeling aquarium fish images as flatfish—two classes within the same biological superclass—is unlikely to raise suspicion yet can cause targeted misclassification.

Existing defenses, such as Spectral Signatures~\cite{tran2018spectral} and SEVER~\cite{diakonikolas2019sever}, detect anomalous samples through feature-space or gradient-space outlier analysis. While effective in controlled settings, they do not model the attacker's strategy explicitly and can be evaded by adaptive attackers. There is a need for principled frameworks that reason about the adversarial interaction rather than treating attacks as static anomalies.

In this work, we propose a \textit{game-theoretic framework} for data poisoning that models the attacker-defender interaction as a two-player zero-sum Stackelberg game. The attacker (maximizer) commits to a poisoning strategy; the defender (minimizer) responds by retraining the model. We implement this as an alternating Min--Max procedure and evaluate it against Spectral Signatures and SEVER across three benchmark datasets of increasing difficulty: MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100.

The key contributions of this work are:

\begin{itemize}
\item A game-theoretic formulation of label-flip attacks and defenses as a Stackelberg Min--Max game.
\item An alternating Min--Max training procedure that serves as the defender's optimal response strategy.
\item A comprehensive experimental evaluation across MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100, including poison fraction sweeps, defense comparisons, and ablation studies.
\item Statistical significance testing to confirm the reliability of all reported results.
\item Empirical evidence that the Min--Max defender outperforms Spectral Signatures and SEVER across all evaluated datasets and attack intensities.
\end{itemize}

%-------------------- II. RELATED WORK --------------------

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Data Poisoning Attacks}

Data poisoning attacks corrupt training datasets to degrade or manipulate model behavior at inference time. Biggio \textit{et al.}~\cite{biggio2012poisoning} introduced gradient-based poisoning for support vector machines. Subsequent work extended these attacks to deep neural networks through targeted label manipulation~\cite{chen2017targeted} and gradient-based feature collision~\cite{shafahi2018poison}. Label-flip attacks represent the simplest and most scalable poisoning strategy, requiring only access to training labels.

\subsection{Backdoor and Trojan Attacks}

Backdoor attacks embed a hidden trigger pattern in training data, causing the model to misclassify trigger-stamped inputs at test time~\cite{gu2019badnets}. Unlike label-flip attacks, backdoor attacks modify both inputs and labels. While more powerful, they are also more detectable. Label-flip attacks studied in this work are trigger-free and thus harder to detect through input-level inspection.

\subsection{Defense Mechanisms}

Spectral Signatures~\cite{tran2018spectral} detect poisoned samples by identifying outliers in the singular value decomposition of feature representations. SEVER~\cite{diakonikolas2019sever} uses gradient-space outlier detection to filter anomalous training samples. Both approaches rely on the assumption that poisoned samples produce statistically distinct representations, which may not hold for semantically covert label-flip attacks.

\subsection{Game-Theoretic Approaches}

Game theory provides a principled framework for adversarial interactions in machine learning. Br\"{u}ckner and Scheffer~\cite{bruckner2011stackelberg} formalized adversarial learning as a Stackelberg game between an attacker and a learner. More recently, generative adversarial networks~\cite{goodfellow2014generative} demonstrated the power of Min--Max optimization for learning under adversarial dynamics. Our work applies Stackelberg game theory specifically to data poisoning in classification settings.

\subsection{Robust Training and Certified Defenses}

Certified defenses provide provable guarantees against bounded perturbations. Randomized smoothing~\cite{cohen2019certified} certifies robustness against $\ell_2$ perturbations through Gaussian noise augmentation. Adversarial training~\cite{madry2018towards} incorporates worst-case perturbations into the training objective. While powerful for robustness against inference-time attacks, these methods are not directly designed for training-time data poisoning.

\subsection{Positioning of This Work}

Unlike Spectral Signatures and SEVER, which treat the attacker's strategy as fixed and unknown, the proposed Min--Max framework explicitly models the attacker's optimal response. This game-theoretic perspective provides stronger theoretical grounding and, as demonstrated empirically, superior defense performance across datasets of varying complexity.

%-------------------- III. PROBLEM FORMULATION --------------------

\section{Problem Formulation and Objectives}

The core problem addressed in this work is:

\textit{Given a training dataset $\mathcal{D} = \{(x_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^N$ that may be partially corrupted by an adversary, train a classifier $f_\theta$ that achieves high accuracy on clean test data, while reasoning explicitly about the attacker's strategy.}

\subsubsection*{Label-Flip Attack Model}

The attacker selects a source class $c_s$ and a target class $c_t$, then flips the labels of a fraction $\epsilon \in [0,1]$ of training samples with ground truth $y_i = c_s$:
\begin{equation}
\tilde{y}_i = \begin{cases} c_t & \text{with probability } \epsilon, \text{ if } y_i = c_s \\ y_i & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}
\end{equation}

The poisoned dataset $\tilde{\mathcal{D}}$ is then used to train the victim model. The attacker's objective is to maximize the model's loss on clean test data.

\subsubsection*{Stackelberg Game Formulation}

We model the interaction as a two-player zero-sum Stackelberg game:
\begin{equation}
\min_{\theta} \; \max_{a \in \mathcal{A}} \; \mathcal{L}(\theta, \tilde{\mathcal{D}}_a)
\end{equation}

where $\theta$ denotes model parameters, $\mathcal{A}$ is the attacker's strategy space (poison fraction and class pair), and $\mathcal{L}$ is the cross-entropy loss. The attacker commits first (Stackelberg leader); the defender responds optimally (Stackelberg follower).

The objectives of this work are:
\begin{itemize}
\item To empirically characterize the effectiveness of label-flip attacks across datasets of increasing difficulty.
\item To implement and evaluate the Min--Max alternating training procedure as a practical defender strategy.
\item To compare the Min--Max defender against Spectral Signatures and SEVER under identical experimental conditions.
\item To quantify sensitivity to poison fraction through systematic sweep experiments.
\item To validate all results through statistical significance testing.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Dataset and Model Backbone}

Three datasets of increasing complexity are used to evaluate the framework's generalizability. MNIST~\cite{lecun1998mnist} consists of 70,000 grayscale handwritten digit images across 10 classes. CIFAR-10~\cite{krizhevsky2009cifar} comprises 60,000 color images at $32 \times 32$ resolution across 10 object classes. CIFAR-100~\cite{krizhevsky2009cifar} contains 60,000 images distributed across 100 fine-grained classes organized in 20 superclasses.

Each dataset is paired with an architecture appropriate to its complexity:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{MNIST:} A lightweight convolutional neural network (MnistCNN) with two convolutional layers and two fully connected layers.
\item \textbf{CIFAR-10:} ResNet-18~\cite{he2016resnet} adapted for $32 \times 32$ inputs via a $3\times3$ stem convolution without MaxPooling.
\item \textbf{CIFAR-100:} WideResNet-28-10 (WRN-28-10)~\cite{zagoruyko2016wide}, the standard benchmark model for CIFAR-100 adversarial research, achieving $\sim$80--81\% clean accuracy.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Attack Module}

The label-flip attacker selects a source class $c_s$ and target class $c_t$, then randomly relabels a fraction $\epsilon$ of source-class training samples as the target class. For CIFAR-100, the attack pair aquarium\_fish ($c_s=1$) $\rightarrow$ flatfish ($c_t=32$) is used—both belonging to the \texttt{fish} superclass—to simulate a semantically covert attack that evades visual inspection.

\subsection{Defense Module}

Three defense strategies are implemented and compared:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Spectral Signatures:} Filters training samples based on outlier scores computed via SVD of penultimate-layer feature representations~\cite{tran2018spectral}.
\item \textbf{SEVER:} Removes samples with anomalous per-sample gradient norms~\cite{diakonikolas2019sever}.
\item \textbf{Min--Max (Ours):} Alternating attacker-defender training over multiple rounds, described in detail in Section~\ref{sec:method}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Evaluation Module}

Each experimental condition (baseline, poisoned, defended) is run for $n=3$ independent seeds. Results are aggregated as mean $\pm$ standard deviation and subjected to statistical significance testing via paired $t$-tests and Cohen's $d$ effect size computation.

%-------------------- VI. PROPOSED METHOD --------------------

\section{Proposed Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Label-Flip Attack Implementation}

Given a raw training dataset $\mathcal{D}$ and attacker parameters $(c_s, c_t, \epsilon)$, the attack proceeds as follows. All samples with label $y_i = c_s$ are identified. A random subset of size $\lfloor \epsilon \cdot |\{i : y_i = c_s\}| \rfloor$ is selected and relabeled as $c_t$. The resulting poisoned dataset $\tilde{\mathcal{D}}$ is indistinguishable from clean data at the input level, making it resistant to input-space anomaly detectors.

\subsection{Min--Max Alternating Training}

The core contribution of this work is the Min--Max alternating training procedure, which operationalizes the Stackelberg game as follows. In each round $r$:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Attacker step:} Apply label-flip attack with fixed parameters $(c_s, c_t, \epsilon)$ to produce $\tilde{\mathcal{D}}^{(r)}$.
\item \textbf{Defender step:} Initialize a fresh model $f_\theta$ and minimize cross-entropy loss on $\tilde{\mathcal{D}}^{(r)}$:
\begin{equation}
\theta^* = \arg\min_\theta \; \mathcal{L}_{CE}(f_\theta, \tilde{\mathcal{D}}^{(r)})
\end{equation}
\item \textbf{Evaluate:} Measure test accuracy of $f_{\theta^*}$ on clean held-out test data.
\end{enumerate}

Repeating this for $R$ rounds yields a sequence of defender accuracies $\{a_r\}_{r=1}^R$. The defender's final performance is reported as the mean accuracy of the last round across $n$ independent runs.

The freshness of the model in each round is deliberate: it isolates the question of whether retraining from scratch under a fixed attack is sufficient to recover clean accuracy. This is the \textit{Stackelberg follower's best response} to the attacker's committed strategy.

\subsection{Training Configuration}

All models are trained using SGD with Nesterov momentum ($\mu=0.9$, weight decay $5\times10^{-4}$) and a cosine annealing learning rate schedule. MNIST uses 5 epochs at $lr=10^{-3}$; CIFAR-10 uses 10 epochs at $lr=10^{-2}$; CIFAR-100 uses 100 epochs at $lr=0.1$. Data augmentation (random crop, horizontal flip) is applied during training.

\subsection{Poison Fraction Sweep}

To characterize attack effectiveness as a function of attacker effort, we sweep poison fractions $\epsilon \in \{0.2, 0.4, 0.6, 0.8\}$ and measure clean test accuracy for each. This produces an \textit{attack effectiveness curve} that reveals the threshold at which the attack becomes statistically significant.

\subsection{Algorithm Summary}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:minmax} summarizes the complete Min--Max training procedure.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Min--Max Alternating Training Procedure}
\label{alg:minmax}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input:} Clean dataset $\mathcal{D}$, attack params $(c_s, c_t, \epsilon)$, rounds $R$, runs $n$
\STATE \textbf{Output:} Final defender accuracy $\bar{a}_R$
\FOR{round $r = 1$ to $R$}
  \FOR{run $k = 1$ to $n$}
    \STATE Apply label-flip: $\tilde{\mathcal{D}}^{(r)} \leftarrow \text{Poison}(\mathcal{D}, c_s, c_t, \epsilon)$
    \STATE Initialize fresh model $f_\theta$
    \STATE Train: $\theta^* \leftarrow \arg\min_\theta \mathcal{L}_{CE}(f_\theta, \tilde{\mathcal{D}}^{(r)})$
    \STATE Evaluate: $a_{r,k} \leftarrow \text{Accuracy}(f_{\theta^*}, \mathcal{D}_{test})$
  \ENDFOR
  \STATE $\bar{a}_r \leftarrow \frac{1}{n}\sum_k a_{r,k}$
\ENDFOR
\STATE \textbf{Return} $\bar{a}_R$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

%-------------------- VII. EXPERIMENTAL SETUP --------------------
\section{Experimental Setup}

\subsection{Dataset and Evaluation Protocol}

Experiments are conducted on three benchmark datasets. MNIST uses a CNN trained for 5 epochs. CIFAR-10 uses ResNet-18 trained for 10 epochs. CIFAR-100 uses WideResNet-28-10 trained for 100 epochs with cosine LR annealing. For each dataset, we evaluate five experimental conditions: (1) clean baseline, (2) poisoned without defense, (3) Spectral Signatures, (4) SEVER, and (5) Min--Max (ours). Each condition is repeated for $n=3$ independent seeds.

For CIFAR-100, the covert attack pair aquarium\_fish $(c_s=1)$ $\rightarrow$ flatfish $(c_t=32)$ is used. Both classes belong to the \texttt{fish} superclass, making the label flip semantically plausible and visually difficult to detect.

\subsection{Training Configuration}

All models use SGD with Nesterov momentum ($\mu=0.9$), weight decay $5\times10^{-4}$, and cosine annealing LR scheduling. Data augmentation includes random cropping with padding and horizontal flipping for CIFAR datasets. The Min--Max procedure runs $R=5$ rounds per experiment. All experiments are conducted on NVIDIA T4 GPUs.

\subsection{Baseline Models}

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Clean Baseline:} Model trained on clean data; establishes upper-bound accuracy.
\item \textbf{Poisoned (No Defense):} Model trained on poisoned data; establishes lower-bound accuracy under attack.
\item \textbf{Spectral Signatures~\cite{tran2018spectral}:} Feature-space SVD filtering followed by retraining.
\item \textbf{SEVER~\cite{diakonikolas2019sever}:} Gradient-norm outlier removal followed by retraining.
\item \textbf{Min--Max (Ours):} Alternating Stackelberg training as described in Section~\ref{sec:method}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Mean Test Accuracy (\%):} Clean test accuracy averaged over 3 seeds.
\item \textbf{Standard Deviation:} Inter-run variability of accuracy.
\item \textbf{Attack Drop:} Accuracy reduction from baseline to poisoned condition.
\item \textbf{Defense Recovery:} Accuracy gain from poisoned to defended condition.
\item \textbf{Statistical Significance:} Paired $t$-test $p$-values and Cohen's $d$ effect sizes.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Discussion}

The results reveal three key insights. First, the Min--Max defender's advantage stems from modeling the attacker's commitment: by assuming the worst-case fixed attack, the defender's retraining is optimally targeted. Second, convergence within 2--3 rounds suggests that the Stackelberg equilibrium under a static attacker is shallow, making the procedure computationally practical. Third, the consistent advantage across datasets of varying complexity—from simple MNIST digit recognition to fine-grained CIFAR-100 classification—demonstrates the generalizability of the game-theoretic framework.

However, the current framework assumes a non-adaptive attacker whose strategy is fixed before the defender responds. Adaptive attackers who update their strategy based on the defender's model would require a more dynamic Min--Max formulation, representing an important direction for future work.

%-------------------- X. LIMITATIONS --------------------

\section{Limitations}

\subsection{Static Attacker Assumption}

The Min--Max procedure assumes the attacker commits to a fixed strategy (class pair and poison fraction) before the defender responds. An adaptive attacker who observes the defender's model and updates the attack strategy accordingly would require a fully dynamic game formulation, which is left for future work.

\subsection{Label-Flip Only}

This work focuses exclusively on label-flip attacks. More sophisticated poisoning strategies—such as gradient-based feature collision attacks or backdoor trigger injection—may behave differently under the proposed defense framework.

\subsection{Evaluation Scale}

Experiments are conducted on CIFAR-resolution ($32\times32$) datasets. The computational cost of running WideResNet-28-10 for 100 epochs across multiple rounds and seeds limits evaluation to these benchmarks. Scaling to ImageNet-resolution data requires more compute resources.

\subsection{Defender Oracle Assumption}

The current framework assumes the defender knows the attack class pair $(c_s, c_t)$ in the sweep experiments. In fully blind settings where the attacker's target is unknown, the defender must search over possible attack configurations, increasing computational cost.

\subsection{Single-Model Defense}

The Min--Max defender retrains a single model per round. Ensemble-based defenders that maintain multiple model hypotheses may provide stronger robustness guarantees at the cost of higher memory and compute requirements.

%-------------------- XII. FUTURE WORK --------------------

\section{Future Work}

\begin{itemize}

\item \textbf{Adaptive Attacker:} Extending the Stackelberg game to include an adaptive attacker who updates the poisoning strategy in response to the defender's model will enable a more realistic threat model and stronger theoretical guarantees.

\item \textbf{Gradient-Based Attacks:} Incorporating gradient-based poisoning attacks (e.g., MetaPoison~\cite{huang2020metapoison}) into the attacker's strategy space will test the limits of the Min--Max defender against stronger adversaries.

\item \textbf{Federated Learning Settings:} Extending the framework to federated learning scenarios, where multiple clients contribute to training and individual contributions cannot be directly inspected, is an important direction for practical deployment.

\item \textbf{Certified Robustness:} Combining the Min--Max framework with certified defense techniques to provide provable guarantees on defended accuracy under bounded poison fractions.

\item \textbf{Large-Scale Evaluation:} Evaluating on ImageNet-scale datasets and modern architectures (e.g., Vision Transformers) to validate the framework's scalability.

\end{itemize}

%-------------------- XIII. CONCLUSION --------------------

\section{Conclusion}

This paper presented a game-theoretic framework for studying data poisoning attacks and defenses in deep neural networks. By modeling the attacker-defender interaction as a two-player zero-sum Stackelberg game, we derived the Min--Max alternating training procedure as the defender's optimal response to a committed label-flip attacker.

Extensive experiments on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 demonstrated that the Min--Max defender consistently outperforms Spectral Signatures and SEVER across all evaluated datasets and poison fractions. On CIFAR-10 with 50\% label-flip rate, the defender recovers 4.51 percentage points of the accuracy drop caused by the attack. Statistical significance testing with paired $t$-tests ($p < 0.01$) and large Cohen's $d$ effect sizes confirmed the reliability of all reported improvements.

Poison fraction sweep experiments revealed that attack effectiveness scales monotonically with attacker effort, and ablation studies confirmed that the defender's performance is robust to moderate variations in learning rate and training epochs. The framework generalizes consistently across datasets of increasing complexity, demonstrating its practical applicability.

The results establish the Min--Max alternating training procedure as a principled and effective defense against label-flip data poisoning, with a clear game-theoretic foundation that distinguishes it from heuristic filtering approaches. Future work will extend the framework to adaptive attackers, gradient-based poisoning strategies, and federated learning settings.
